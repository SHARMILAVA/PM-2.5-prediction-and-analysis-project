What Is PM2.5?

PM2.5 refers to fine particulate matter with diameter less than 2.5 micrometers. These particles can penetrate deep into lungs and enter the bloodstream, increasing respiratory and cardiovascular risk.

Major Causes

Common causes include vehicle exhaust, industrial emissions, fossil-fuel combustion, construction dust, crop residue burning, and secondary atmospheric reactions under stagnant weather conditions.

How Parameters Affect Prediction

Higher haze score and turbidity generally indicate heavier aerosol loading. Lower visibility and contrast often indicate denser particulate presence. Brightness and saturation shifts can reflect scattering and atmospheric pollution intensity.

Health Advisory Concept

As PM2.5 levels rise, precaution intensity increases: reduce outdoor exertion, use protective masks in polluted zones, and prioritize indoor ventilation control for sensitive groups.

Environmental Context

Meteorology influences PM2.5 significantly. Temperature inversion, low wind speed, and dry-season dust accumulation can increase pollution persistence and reduce dispersion.

Interpretation Note

Predictions are decision-support indicators based on image-derived features. Final policy action should combine these outputs with calibrated monitoring station observations.
